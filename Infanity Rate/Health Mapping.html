This project explores spatial inequality in health outcomes across Ontario through two linked analyses: a choropleth mapping of infant mortality rates by sex, and a Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) cluster analysis of child and senior road collision rates.

Together, the two parts reveal how geographic and demographic vulnerability intersect across the province's public health regions and dissemination areas.

Data Source   Statistics Canada, Ontario Road Safety Annual Report

Projection   NAD 1983 Statistics Canada Lambert

Tools   ArcGIS Pro — Choropleth, Spatial Weights, LISA

Course   GGR376, University of Toronto

Part 1 — Infant Mortality

Classification &
Data Join

Tabular Join

Infant mortality rate data were joined to the Ontario public health region boundaries using a common PHU_ID field. After joining, null values were inspected and records without matching spatial features were excluded to ensure cartographic accuracy.

Classification Decision

To enable direct visual comparison between male and female infant mortality rates, a unified breakpoint scheme was adopted. Class boundaries were derived from the male IMR quartile distribution — the sex with a higher range and greater variance — and applied identically to the female map. This ensures that colour gradations represent equivalent rate thresholds across both maps, making gender disparities immediately readable without rescaling artefacts.

Part 1 — Key Findings

North–South Gradient
& Gender Gap

Geographic Pattern

A clear north–south gradient characterises Ontario's infant mortality landscape. Remote northern regions — particularly those with large Indigenous populations — consistently record the highest rates, while the Greater Toronto Area and surrounding public health units occupy the lowest quintile. This spatial pattern reflects well-documented structural inequities in healthcare access, socioeconomic conditions, and social determinants of health.

Gender Disparity

Male infant mortality rates averaged 10.8 per 1,000 live births against 8.2 per 1,000 for females — a consistent gap observed across virtually every region. The disparity is most pronounced in northern health units, suggesting that geographic disadvantage amplifies sex-based biological differences in infant survival.

 

"Northern and remote regions carry a disproportionate burden of infant mortality — a pattern that persists across sex and reflects structural, not merely biological, inequality."

Part 1 — Interpretation
GTA Pattern

The Greater Toronto Area records among the lowest infant mortality rates provincially for both sexes, consistent with its concentration of tertiary healthcare infrastructure and higher median household income.

Part 2 — Collision Clusters

Standardised Rate
& Spatial Weights

Standardised Collision Rate

Raw collision counts were standardised to account for population size differences across dissemination areas. A per-1,000 rate was computed separately for child and senior populations:

Formula

Child Rate  =  Child_Inj / ChildPop × 1,000

Senior Rate  =  Senior_Inj / SeniorPop × 1,000

Dissemination areas with a denominator population of zero were assigned null values and excluded from the analysis to prevent division artefacts from distorting cluster detection.

Spatial Weights Matrix

A Queen's case contiguity weights matrix was constructed, defining neighbours as any dissemination area sharing at least one boundary point. Row-standardised weights ensure that areas with many neighbours are not systematically down-weighted, preserving the interpretability of the local Moran's I statistic.

Part 2 — LISA Results

Local Spatial
Autocorrelation

Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) identifies statistically significant clusters and spatial outliers at the dissemination area level, revealing where high or low collision rates co-occur with similarly rated neighbours.

LISA Cluster Categories
High–High — high rate surrounded by high rates
Low–Low — low rate surrounded by low rates
High–Low — high rate surrounded by low rates
Low–High — low rate surrounded by high rates
Not Significant — no significant spatial pattern
Child Collision Clusters

High–High clusters of child collision rates concentrate in inner-city neighbourhoods and older suburban arterial corridors, where traffic volumes, pedestrian exposure, and limited traffic-calming infrastructure intersect. Low–Low zones dominate lower-density outer suburbs and rural areas, reflecting both reduced exposure and lower pedestrian activity.

Part 2 — Senior Analysis

Senior Collision
Clusters

Senior Cluster Pattern

Senior collision clusters exhibit a markedly different spatial signature from child clusters. High–High zones are dispersed across inner suburban areas with high concentrations of older housing stock and mixed land use, rather than being tightly centralised. Seniors' reliance on surface crossings and lower pedestrian agility likely amplifies risk at mid-density intersections rather than in the denser urban core.

Comparative Discussion

The divergence between child and senior cluster maps underscores that road safety risk is age-stratified and cannot be addressed through a uniform spatial intervention. Children are most vulnerable in high-traffic urban cores; seniors face elevated risk in transitional suburban zones with high crossing distances and lower signal density. Targeted interventions — traffic calming near schools versus extended pedestrian signal phases near senior-oriented facilities — are warranted by the evidence.
