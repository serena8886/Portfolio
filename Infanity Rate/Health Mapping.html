Part 1 — Infant Mortality

Data Join &
Classification

Tabular Join

Infant mortality rate data were joined to Ontario public health region boundaries using a shared PHU_ID field. Null values were inspected post-join and unmatched records excluded to maintain cartographic accuracy across all health unit polygons.

Classification Decision

A unified breakpoint scheme was adopted to enable direct side-by-side comparison of male and female IMR maps. Class boundaries were derived from the male IMR quartile distribution — the sex exhibiting higher range and greater variance — then applied identically to the female layer.

This approach ensures colour gradations represent equivalent rate thresholds across both maps, making gender disparities immediately legible without rescaling artefacts or perceptual mismatches between the two layouts.

Part 1 — Rationale

Why Unified
Breakpoints?

The Problem with Independent Classification

If male and female maps used independently derived class breaks, identical colours would represent different rate ranges — making a direct visual comparison misleading. A region appearing "medium-high" on both maps could have rates differing by several points per 1,000 live births.

Anchoring to Male IMR

Male infant mortality rates span a wider range provincially, making their quartile intervals a more robust anchor. By standardising to these breaks, the female map preserves full comparability — the same colour always means the same rate, regardless of sex — and the gender gap becomes directly readable from colour contrast alone.

Part 1 — Key Findings

North–South Gradient
& Gender Gap

Geographic Pattern

A clear north–south gradient characterises Ontario's infant mortality landscape. Remote northern regions — particularly those with large Indigenous populations — consistently record the highest rates, while the Greater Toronto Area and surrounding public health units occupy the lowest quintile. This spatial pattern reflects structural inequities in healthcare access, socioeconomic conditions, and social determinants of health.

Gender Disparity

Male infant mortality rates averaged 10.8 per 1,000 live births against 8.2 per 1,000 for females — a consistent gap observed across virtually every region. The disparity is most pronounced in northern health units, suggesting that geographic disadvantage amplifies biological differences in infant survival.

 

"Northern and remote regions carry a disproportionate burden of infant mortality — a pattern that persists across sex and reflects structural, not merely biological, inequality."

Part 1 — Interpretation
GTA Pattern

The Greater Toronto Area records among the lowest infant mortality rates provincially for both sexes, consistent with its concentration of tertiary healthcare infrastructure and higher median household income.

Part 2 — Collision Clusters

Standardised Rate
& Spatial Weights

Standardised Collision Rate

Raw collision counts were standardised to account for population size differences across dissemination areas. A per-1,000 rate was computed separately for child and senior populations:

Formula

Child Rate  =  Child_Inj / ChildPop × 1,000

Senior Rate  =  Senior_Inj / SeniorPop × 1,000

Dissemination areas with a zero denominator were assigned null values and excluded to prevent division artefacts from distorting cluster detection.

Part 2 — Spatial Structure

Spatial Weights
Matrix

Queen's Case Contiguity

A Queen's case contiguity weights matrix was constructed, defining neighbours as any dissemination area sharing at least one boundary point — including corner-touching polygons. This is more inclusive than Rook's contiguity and better captures realistic spatial adjacency in irregular census geometries.

Row Standardisation

Weights were row-standardised so that each area's neighbours sum to 1.0. This prevents areas with many neighbours from being systematically down-weighted, preserving the interpretability of the local Moran's I statistic across the study area.

Part 2 — LISA Results

Local Spatial
Autocorrelation

LISA identifies statistically significant clusters and spatial outliers at the dissemination area level, revealing where high or low collision rates co-occur with similarly rated neighbours — and where they do not.

LISA Cluster Categories
High–High   high rate, high-rate neighbours
Low–Low   low rate, low-rate neighbours
High–Low   high rate, low-rate neighbours
Low–High   low rate, high-rate neighbours
Not Significant   no spatial pattern detected

Part 2 — Child Clusters

Child Collision
Findings

High–High Zones

High–High clusters of child collision rates concentrate in inner-city neighbourhoods and older suburban arterial corridors, where traffic volumes, pedestrian exposure, and limited traffic-calming infrastructure converge.

Low–Low Zones

Low–Low zones dominate lower-density outer suburbs and rural areas, reflecting both reduced traffic exposure and lower pedestrian activity among the child population.

Spatial Outliers

High–Low outliers in peri-urban areas suggest localised risk hotspots not embedded in broader clusters — often corresponding to school zones or major intersections within otherwise low-risk suburban fabric.

Part 2 — Senior Analysis

Senior Collision
Clusters

Senior Cluster Pattern

Senior collision clusters exhibit a markedly different spatial signature. High–High zones are dispersed across inner suburban areas with mixed land use, rather than being tightly centralised in the urban core. Seniors' reliance on surface crossings and lower pedestrian agility amplifies risk at mid-density intersections.

Comparative Discussion

The divergence between child and senior cluster maps confirms that road safety risk is age-stratified and cannot be addressed through a uniform spatial intervention. Children are most vulnerable in high-traffic urban cores; seniors face elevated risk in transitional suburban zones with long crossing distances and lower signal density.

Part 2 — Implications

Targeted
Interventions

Children

Traffic calming measures — speed reductions, raised crosswalks, and reduced signal cycle lengths — are most warranted near school zones and dense urban arterials where child High–High clusters are concentrated.

Seniors

Extended pedestrian signal phases, improved crossing visibility, and refuge islands are indicated near senior-oriented facilities in inner suburban areas where senior High–High clusters appear.

Conclusion

Together, the two LISA maps demonstrate that spatial cluster analysis is an essential tool for age-sensitive road safety planning — one that reveals patterns invisible to aggregate statistics.
