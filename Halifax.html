Halifax

Light Rail Transit Proposal

A GIS-based multi-criteria analysis identifying an optimal Light Rail Transit corridor connecting suburban Halifax to the downtown train station. Using ArcGIS Pro, an impedance surface was constructed from terrain, hydrology, wetland sensitivity, commuter density, and existing bus network proximity across the Halifax Regional Municipality.

Impedance Criteria
  • Terrain slope (LiDAR DEM)
  • Distance to water bodies
  • Distance to wetlands
  • Commuter density by dissemination area
  • Proximity to existing transit routes
Location Halifax Regional Municipality
Nova Scotia, Canada

Part I  ·  Original Analysis

Four-Criteria
Impedance Surface

The initial least cost path was derived from a composite raster integrating four environmental and demographic factors. Cell impedance values ranged from 125 to 17,025, with the path corridor favouring low-slope, populated areas clear of hydrological features.

Methodology
  • Slope reclassified from LiDAR DEM (1m resolution)
  • Euclidean distance to lakes and rivers
  • Euclidean distance to wetland polygons
  • Commuter density from 2021 Census DA data
  • Rasters summed and normalised as Raster_TOTAL

The resulting route traced populated commuter corridors while avoiding steep terrain and sensitive hydrological features — establishing the baseline alignment for further refinement.

Part II  ·  Expanded Analysis

"Multimodal integration is not an addendum to transit planning — it is the condition upon which a route earns its right of way."

— Halifax Rapid Transit Strategy, 2020

Bus Network
Integration

A fifth criterion — proximity to existing Halifax Transit bus routes — was introduced as a multimodal connectivity layer. Stops within 200 m received the lowest impedance penalty; beyond 1,000 m the cost rose sharply to 2,000.

The Part 2 route diverged only marginally from Part 1, confirming that existing bus corridors already occupy low-impedance terrain. The expanded alignment is recommended for its stronger alignment with Halifax's Rapid Transit Strategy and its potential to reduce car dependency across suburban commuter zones.
